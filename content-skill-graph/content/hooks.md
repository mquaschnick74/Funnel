# Opening Line Rules — The Image Diagnostic

## The Core Rule

**A first line that can be paraphrased is a thesis. A first line that can only be seen is an image.**

This is the most important rule in this entire system.

## The Diagnostic Test

Ask yourself: Can I restate this opening in different words and keep the same meaning?

- If YES → it's a thesis. Rewrite.
- If NO → it's an image. Proceed.

### Examples

**Thesis (wrong):**
"Many people struggle to access mental health care."
→ Can be paraphrased: "Lots of folks have trouble seeing a therapist." Same information. Fails the test.

**Image (right):**
"She sat in her car in the parking lot for ten minutes before driving the three hours home."
→ Cannot be paraphrased without losing the picture. No restatement preserves the specific moment. Passes the test.

**Thesis (wrong):**
"The mental health system is failing millions of Americans."
→ Can be paraphrased endlessly. Fails immediately.

**Image (right):**
"The voicemail said: 'We're not accepting new patients at this time. Please try again in six months.'"
→ You see it. You hear it. You cannot restate it. Passes.

## Ending Rule

The closing line must match the specificity of the opening. It cannot drift into literary abstraction or broad statement after opening with something concrete.

**Wrong ending (after a specific opening):**
"We all deserve to be heard." ← too abstract, too generic

**Right ending:**
"She never called back." ← specific, closes the loop, does not reach for meaning

## What Makes a Good Physical Image

- A specific action: "He closed the browser tab."
- A specific sound: "The hold music cut out."
- A specific place: "The waiting room had six chairs and a plant that had seen better days."
- A specific number used humanly: "47 minutes on hold."
- A specific failure: "Page not found."

## The Failure Mode to Watch For

Posts that START with an image but drift into thesis territory by the third line. Every line must earn its specificity. The moment you reach for a broad statement — stop.
